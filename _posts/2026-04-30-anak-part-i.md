**Part I - The Beautiful Empty**

*"Hope deferred makes the heart sick."*
*Proverbs 13:12*

---

The apartment belonged to a couple who had everything except time for each other.

Chel could tell from the brief: *open plan, clean lines, we entertain a lot.* From the space itself, which was large and north-facing and had never learned what it was for. She stood in the middle of it with her sketchpad and let it speak. Rooms always spoke if you were quiet enough. This one said: *we are performing a life here.* It said: *make it look like we know what we want.*

She could do that. She was good at that.

She moved through the empty rooms with the particular attention she gave to other people's spaces, measuring light, reading proportions, noting where the eye wanted to rest and where it was pushed away. The master bedroom faced east. Good bones, terrible curtains. A built-in wardrobe that had been added later and never quite forgiven for it. She made notes in the margins of her sketchpad, quick and sure, the pencil moving without hesitation.

This was the part of the work she loved. Before the client's preferences arrived and narrowed everything down. Just her and the space and the question of what it could become.

Her phone buzzed. Jacob, a single line: *appointment at 2. don't be late.*

She looked at the message for a moment. Then put the phone face down on the windowsill and went back to her notes.

---

The clinic was in the kind of building that tried too hard to be calming. Pale timber. Soft lighting. A print of eucalyptus branches above the reception desk that Chel had noticed on every visit and had never been able to decide if she found restful or not.

Jacob was already there when she arrived, jacket on, phone in hand, the particular set to his jaw that meant he had been waiting longer than he thought he should have. He stood when he saw her, slipping the phone into his pocket.

"How was the site?"

"Good. Big space."

"Good." He said it the way he said most things lately, efficiently, one word doing the work of several.

They sat beside each other in the waiting room chairs, close enough to touch but not touching. Chel folded her hands in her lap.

When the doctor called them in she stood up before Jacob did.

---

The office was small, lit too bright. A monitor on the desk showed an image Chel didn't look at directly. The doctor had a clipboard and the careful manner of someone who had delivered this kind of news before and had learned that there was no good way to do it.

He set the clipboard down and looked at them both for a moment before he spoke. Not at his notes. At them.

"I'm very sorry," he said. "Genuinely." A pause that wasn't procedural, just a man acknowledging that he had said something that would change the shape of their entire future. "None of the eggs fertilized. This is what we call a total fertilization failure. There were no embryos."

The room was very quiet.

Chel heard the words and felt them arrive in some part of her that was below language, below thought. They settled there without echo. *There were no embryos.* Clean and final, the way a door closing is final.

Beside her Jacob leaned forward. "So we try again. Change the protocol. There are other options."

The doctor nodded slowly. "It's possible to try again. But in cases like this, the chances of success are significantly reduced."

"Reduced isn't impossible."

"Jacob," Chel said. Quietly. Just his name.

He stopped. Looked at her. Something moved across his face and was gone.

"I'd encourage you both to take some time," the doctor said. "My office will be in touch about next steps whenever you're ready. There's no rush."

Whenever you're ready. As though readiness were a thing you could arrive at.

Chel stood before either of them had finished speaking.

She walked back through the waiting room without stopping. The eucalyptus print still hung at a slightly wrong angle above the reception desk. She had noticed it on every visit. Nobody had fixed it. The world had no interest in being tidy today.

---

Outside, the street was ordinary and indifferent the way streets are after the worst news: trams running, people walking, a food truck on the corner already doing lunch trade. Chel stood on the pavement and let it happen around her.

Jacob came out behind her. He reached for her shoulder.

She stepped forward, just slightly, and his hand found air.

She didn't mean it as a statement. She just needed, for a moment, not to be touched.

They walked to the car without speaking. The drive home was twenty minutes and neither of them found anything to say that was equal to the silence, so they left it alone.

---

The townhouse was pale stone and clean lines, a Fitzroy terrace she had renovated three years ago with the same careful attention she gave to clients' homes. She stood in the entrance hall now and felt it receive her the way it always did, beautiful, ordered, and somehow beside the point.

Jacob set his keys on the counter. Loosened his collar. Opened the fridge and stood in front of it without taking anything out.

"We should eat something," he said.

"I'm not hungry."

He closed the fridge. Turned to look at her across the kitchen. In the late afternoon light he looked tired in a way that went past the day, the kind of tired that had been accumulating for longer than either of them had been willing to name.

"We're not finished," he said. "This isn't the end."

Chel looked at him. At the way he was standing, shoulders back, jaw set, already moving toward the next thing. Already building the case for persistence. She had loved that about him once. The absolute certainty that if you kept working, kept pushing, kept refusing to accept the shape of a problem as fixed, things could be moved.

"I know," she said.

She went upstairs.

---

She lay on top of the covers in the last of the daylight, still dressed, arms at her sides. The ceiling was white and even and offered nothing back. She thought about the apartment she had been standing in that morning, the north-facing light, the wardrobe that didn't belong, the couple who entertained a lot. She thought about how she would make it warm for them. How she would give it a logic it didn't currently have, a sense that the people who lived there had chosen each detail because it meant something.

She was good at that. Making spaces feel like someone had thought carefully about what they were for.

She pressed her palms flat against her stomach.

After a while she reached for her sketchbook from the bedside table. Not to work, just to have something in her hands. She opened it to the middle, somewhere neutral, a page of client notes and measurements she barely registered.

She turned a page.

A cradle. Drawn in her own hand, her own line weight, the proportions careful and considered. The canopy curved softly overhead, the bars spaced with the same eye for rhythm she brought to everything she designed. She stared at it for a long moment.

She had no memory of drawing it.

She turned back a page, then forward again, as though the act of looking away and returning might change what was there. It didn't. The cradle sat on the page with the quiet authority of something that had always been there and simply waited to be found.

She closed the book. Set it back on the bedside table.

After a while she heard Jacob moving downstairs. The quiet opening and closing of cupboards. Then music, low, his way of filling a room when he didn't have words for it. Something slow, a voice she recognized without naming. Neo-soul, the kind they had listened to together in the early years, driving nowhere in particular, the windows down. She lay still and let it come up through the floorboards.

Then she reached for her laptop.

---

She found herself at the dining table with the call connecting before she had fully decided to make it. The screen filled with her parents' living room, the familiar arrangement of furniture she hadn't sat in for two years, her mother already smiling before the image had fully resolved, her father a step behind as always, settling into his chair with the deliberate manner of a man who treated video calls as formal occasions.

Behind them, slightly left of center, the crucifix. It was always there.

"Anak." Her mother's face opened into the particular warmth she reserved for Chel, her eldest, the one who had gone furthest. "You look tired."

"I'm fine, Mama. Just a long day."

Her father leaned forward, studying her through the screen with the focused attention he had once turned on engineering problems. "Kumusta si Jacob?"

"He's good. He's here." She half-turned. Jacob was at the kitchen counter, back to her, scrolling his phone. She didn't call him over.

Her mother's eyes moved across the space behind Chel, the clean lines of the kitchen, the orchids on the sill, the particular tidiness of a home without children. Isabel had a way of seeing everything without appearing to look.

"You're eating properly?" her mother asked.

"Yes, Mama."

"You don't look like you're eating properly."

"Mama."

Her father smiled. Her mother pressed on, as she always did, because love in their household had always expressed itself as vigilance. Had Chel seen a doctor recently. Was the Melbourne winter too cold. Had she and Jacob managed to take any time off.

Then, gently, because her mother had never been anything but gentle about this, Isabel folded her hands and said: "And when will you give us our apo, anak? We're not getting any younger."

The words arrived in Chel's chest and sat there.

She was aware of several things at once: the faint hum of the refrigerator, Jacob's stillness at the counter, the slight delay on the call that made her parents' faces flicker almost imperceptibly. The crucifix on the wall. Her mother's expression, open, hopeful, entirely without suspicion that anything was wrong.

"We're working on it, Mama," she said. Her voice came out steady. She had become practiced at steady.

Her mother's face softened further. "In God's time, anak. Life is a gift. You can't rush a gift."

*In God's time.* Chel looked at her mother's face on the screen and thought about the doctor's office, the monitor she hadn't looked at directly, the words *there were no embryos* still cycling somewhere underneath everything else.

"I know, Mama."

Her mother reached toward the camera the way she always did, the gesture that had always been slightly heartbreaking, the impulse to touch across twelve thousand kilometers of distance, the hand finding only glass.

"Kumusta ka talaga, anak." Her voice had dropped. Not suspicious. Just knowing, the way mothers knew. "How are you really."

Chel opened her mouth.

Felt the full weight of what she was not saying, not just today's news, not just the months of trying, but the older thing, the thing that sat underneath all of it like a stone beneath shallow water.

"I'm just tired, Mama," she said. "Work has been busy."

Her mother looked at her for a moment longer than necessary. Then nodded, accepting it, because Chel had never given her reason not to.

They talked for another ten minutes about small things, her father's garden, a cousin's wedding, the parish's fiesta preparations. Chel smiled when she was supposed to smile and laughed when she was supposed to laugh and said *po* and *opo* automatically, the habit of respect so deep it survived everything.

When the call ended she sat for a moment with the dark screen reflecting her face back at her.

Jacob had moved to the table while she was talking. He set a glass of water beside her without comment and went back to the kitchen.

Chel looked at the water. Then at the orchids on the windowsill, their petals perfect and closed.

*Life is a gift*, her mother had said.

She sat with that for a while in the quiet of the townhouse, turning it over, feeling its edges. Then she closed the laptop and went upstairs.

---

Later, much later, the house fully dark, Jacob asleep beside her with one arm across his chest, Chel lay awake and looked at the ceiling and did not pray, exactly, but held a kind of attention in the direction of something she couldn't name.

*There were no embryos.*

The words had been moving through her all evening, surfacing and sinking, surfacing again. Clean and clinical and underneath them something else entirely, something older and less ordered, a grief that didn't have the right shape yet because she hadn't been given permission to feel it fully.

She thought: *I should call Mai tomorrow.*

She thought: *I am so tired.*

She thought: *I don't know who I'm talking to but something is wrong and I can't carry it alone.*

And then, beneath the thought, beneath the tiredness, beneath the clinical words still cycling through her, *she heard it*.

Faint. Inside the walls. Not quite a cry. More like the memory of one, or the idea of one, thin and unresolved, the sound a cry makes in the moment before it becomes itself.

Chel went still.

The sound continued for three, four seconds. Then stopped.

The silence that followed was different from the silence before it. Fuller. As though something had just become aware that she was listening.

She sat up.

The room was dark and undisturbed. Jacob's breathing steady beside her. The streetlight laying its usual pale geometry across the ceiling.

Nothing.

She waited, breath held, for the sound to come again.

It didn't.

She told herself: grief does strange things to the mind. She told herself: the house has old pipes, the terrace next door has a newborn, sound travels in ways you don't always understand.

She told herself it was nothing.

But she did not lie back down for a long time. And when she finally did, she lay facing the door, eyes open, listening to the specific quality of the silence, the way it pressed back, the way it held its breath, until the grey of early morning began to show at the edges of the curtains.

And in all that time, the sound did not come again.

Which was almost worse.
