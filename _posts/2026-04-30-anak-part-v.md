## Part V - You Were Sure

> "For there is nothing hidden that will not be disclosed, and nothing concealed that will not be known or brought out into the open."
> *Luke 8:17*

---

They did not speak about it that night.

They sat in the bedroom until the dark outside the curtains softened to grey, Jacob's arm around her, Chel's hand pressed against his chest. Not sleeping. Not talking. Just present in the way that people are present after something that cannot be undone has happened, when words are not yet equal to what they've both seen.

In the morning Jacob made coffee. Chel came downstairs and found two cups on the counter, his already half-drunk, him standing at the kitchen window looking at the garden with the expression he had worn since they got home from the party, the one that had no protocol behind it.

She stood in the kitchen doorway and looked at him and understood that something had shifted in the geometry of the house. The cold distance of the months before was gone. What had replaced it was not warmth exactly. Something rawer than warmth. Two people in the same room who had both seen the same thing and were still figuring out what it meant for everything else.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

He turned from the window. He didn't say anything. He waited.

Chel wrapped both hands around her cup. She had rehearsed this in the dark while he slept and none of the rehearsals had felt right and this version would not feel right either, but she was done waiting for the right version.

"What's been happening," she said. "In the house. I think I know why."

Jacob's jaw tightened fractionally. "Chel—"

"Let me finish."

He was quiet.

She told him. The pregnancy, early in their marriage, before they had built what they thought they needed first. The Philippines. The woman they went to. She watched the word land on him and watched him hold very still around it the way you hold still around something that might break if you move.

"We dealt with it," he said. His voice was careful. Flat.

"We ended it," she said. "In the Philippines. Because we didn't want anyone to know." She looked at her coffee. "I didn't know how to say no to you. You were so sure."

The kitchen was very quiet.

"Chel." His voice had changed, something pressed down inside it. "That was—we agreed. We both—"

"I know we agreed." She looked at him. "I'm telling you what it felt like. I'm telling you what I've been carrying."

He opened his mouth. Closed it. Looked away.

"I think what's in this house," she said, "knows what we did. I think that's why it came here. I think that's why it knows my voice."

Jacob pressed a fist against his mouth. His eyes were fixed on the garden outside, the clean lines of it, the low-maintenance order of it. He stood very still for a long time.

"I need some air," he said.

He put his cup down and walked out of the kitchen. She heard the front door open and close.

She stood alone in the kitchen with her coffee going cold between her hands and looked at the orchids on the sill, their petals perfect and closed, and did not cry.

---

He came back two hours later.

She didn't ask where he had been. He sat down across from her at the dining table, not beside her, and looked at his hands for a long moment.

"I spoke to mum," he said.

Chel looked at him.

"I didn't know who else to talk to."

She nodded. She didn't ask what Cecilia had said. She could see it in him anyway, something slightly less locked than it had been when he left, the way a room looks after a window has been opened.

They sat together in the quiet of the townhouse for a while, not talking, and it was not the cold silence of before. It was the silence of two people who have said the hard thing and are now sitting in the space it made.

---

Mai arrived that evening with a bag over one shoulder and a container of her mother's chicken adobo, which she set on the kitchen counter without comment. She had not asked whether she was needed. She had simply known.

Chel waited until Jacob had gone to bed. Then she and Mai sat at the dining table with tea, the table between them, and Chel told her everything.

She started at the beginning. Early in their marriage, before they had built what they thought they needed first, the pregnancy she and Jacob had discovered. The panic of it. The families they could not tell. Jacob's certainty that the timing was wrong, that they had their whole lives ahead of them, that there would be time. Her own fear, quieter than his but just as present, the fear of disappointing the people who had raised her, the fear of derailing everything she had worked for. The way his certainty had felt like solid ground when she had none of her own.

"We went to a woman in Jacob's province," Chel said. "They said she was an albularyo. Herbal remedies. Natural. They said it would be safe."

Mai was very still across the table.

"She wasn't an albularyo," Chel said. "We didn't know that then. Something about the place felt wrong. The ritual. The way she looked at me afterward. We didn't ask questions because we didn't want to know the answers."

"I think she wanted what we brought her," Chel said. "I think that's what this is. What was born from it."

The kitchen was very quiet. Outside the window the garden was dark, the clean lines of it lost in the night.

Mai reached across the table and covered Chel's hands with hers. Her eyes were wet.

"Ate," she said softly.

"I know," Chel said.

"No." Mai's grip tightened. "I mean, I'm here. I'm right here. You should have told me."

Chel's throat tightened. "I didn't know how."

"I know." Mai was quiet for a moment. "I know."

She didn't preach. She didn't offer explanations or remedies or the careful comfort of someone managing another person's pain from a safe distance. She just sat with her hands over Chel's in the quiet kitchen and let it be as heavy as it was.

After a long time she said: "What the woman did, using the life for something dark. That's not on you. You didn't know."

"We chose not to ask."

"Yes," Mai said simply. She didn't argue with it. "And you've been carrying that."

Chel nodded.

"And the thing in this house," Mai said carefully, "you think it came through what she did."

"I think she opened something. And it found us." Chel looked at her sister. "It knows things only I know. It knows my voice. It spoke to Jacob in his own words." She paused. "It knew the date."

Mai crossed herself quietly, almost automatically, the gesture so habitual she barely seemed to notice she had done it.

"The scapular Lola Coring gave you," Mai said. "Where is it?"

"Upstairs. On the bedside table."

"Keep it on you," Mai said. "From now on. Don't put it down."

Chel nodded.

Mai refilled her tea without asking. They sat together until the kitchen light felt less thin, until the weight in the room had shifted from unbearable to merely present, the way weight does when it is finally shared between more than one person.

---

Saturday evening.

Jacob had been different since the phone call with Cecilia, quieter in a way that was not the old coldness but something new, something that was paying attention. He had not raised the Sydney clinic again. He had not used the word protocol. He moved through the house more carefully, as though the house itself required a different kind of attention than he had been giving it.

Mai was in the spare room. The three of them had eaten dinner together, the conversation careful and warm in the way of people who are being gentle with each other around something large. Jacob had done the dishes without being asked. Chel had watched him at the sink and thought about the glass of water he used to set beside her during her parents' calls, the small wordless gestures that had always been there even when everything else had gone cold.

She was on the sofa. Jacob came in and sat beside her, not at the far end, beside her, close enough that their arms almost touched. He didn't say anything. After a moment he reached for his phone, opened a music app, and set it on the coffee table.

Neo-soul. Something slow and low, a voice she recognized without needing to name. The kind of music they had listened to in the early years, driving nowhere in particular, the windows down. She hadn't *heard it* in the house in a long time, not like this, not with both of them in the room and nothing between them but the music.

They sat in the quiet warmth of it for a while.

Then the temperature dropped.

It was physical, immediate, the way cold arrives in a room when a window opens somewhere. Chel went still. Beside her she felt Jacob go still too.

It was in the corner by the door. Standing. The size of a toddler, the proportions slightly wrong in the way she knew now, the head bowed forward, the face in shadow. Still, the way it was always still, with the patience of something that had nowhere else to be.

She did not move. Jacob did not move.

Then she felt it beside her. Jacob's arms beginning to rise.

She turned to look at him. His face was very pale, his eyes fixed on the corner, and his arms were lifting in front of him with the slow involuntary quality of something the body does before the mind can stop it. The gesture of a man reaching for a child. His hands extended toward it, open, trembling slightly.

The tiyanak moved.

Not scuttling, not sudden. Slowly, deliberately, the way a child moves toward someone it trusts, it stepped out of the corner. Toward him. Toward his open hands. Its face still in shadow, its head tilting upward as it came, as though looking for his eyes.

Something broke open in her chest.

She reached out and took his hands. Pulled them down hard. His fingers closed around hers and held on, gripping tight, and she felt the shudder move through him as the motion was arrested, as his body understood what his mind had almost let happen.

"Anak," he said.

The word came out of him like something involuntary, like the arms had been. She wasn't sure he knew he had said it. His voice was wrecked, stripped of everything he used it for.

The tiyanak stopped. It looked at their joined hands. Then its gaze moved to Jacob and its mouth opened and the voice that came out was the voice from the mirror, almost his but not quite, wrong in the way a reflection is wrong.

*"You were sure."*

Jacob made a sound beside her. Not a word. Something below words, something that had been waiting a long time to come out. The sound of something landing that had been falling for years without hitting ground.

The corner was empty. Between one breath and the next, it was just a corner again, pale wall and shadow, ordinary.

The music was still playing. Something slow and aching, a voice asking a question the song never quite answered.

They sat with their hands locked together for a long time without speaking.

Then Jacob said, not to the room, not to the music, not to the dark:

"I'm sorry."

His voice was very quiet. Raw at the edges. Not the careful steadiness of a man managing a situation. Just his voice, stripped of everything he had been using it for.

Chel looked at him. His eyes were wet. He didn't look away.

"I'm sorry," he said again. "I was so sure. And I shouldn't have—I should have asked you what you wanted. I should have asked more than once."

She didn't say *it's okay.* It wasn't okay. It had never been okay and they both knew it and the knowing was, finally, something they were doing together.

She leaned her head against his shoulder. He put his arm around her. They sat in the dark with the music playing and the house quiet around them and outside the street went on without them, indifferent and ordinary, as it always did.

After a while Jacob said, quietly, into her hair:

"We need to go back."

Chel closed her eyes.

"I know," she said.
