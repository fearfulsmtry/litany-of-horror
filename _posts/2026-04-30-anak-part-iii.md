**Part III - A Meaningful Change in Variables**

*"O my God, I cry by day, but you do not answer; and by night, but find no rest."*
*Psalm 22:2*

---

Jacob found the research on a Sunday morning.

Chel heard him from the kitchen, the particular quality of his focus audible in his silence, the way the apartment held its breath when he was deep in something. She came to the doorway with her coffee and found him at the table, a dozen tabs open, a notepad beside the laptop with figures she couldn't read from where she stood.

"There's a clinic in Sydney," he said, without looking up. "Different protocol. Higher success rates with unexplained fertilization failure." He tapped the notepad. "We'd need to adjust the hormone regimen, probably start from scratch with the workup, but if the issue is the stimulation rather than the eggs themselves—"

Chel looked at him. At the notepad. At the dozen browser tabs.

"Jacob."

"—then a different approach could change the outcome entirely. It's not the same as trying the same thing again. This would be a meaningful change in variables."

*A meaningful change in variables.*

She set her coffee down on the counter behind her. The sound was quiet but he looked up at it.

"We should at least look at it," he said. His voice had the careful steadiness of someone who had been rehearsing. "I'm not saying it's guaranteed. I'm saying the data supports—"

"I know," she said.

She went upstairs.

She sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the window and did not cry, which surprised her. She had expected to cry. Instead there was just the quiet, and the particular quality of the light through the curtains, and the faint sound of Jacob downstairs closing one browser tab and opening another.

He came up twenty minutes later. Stood in the doorway with his hands in his pockets, the notepad gone, face careful.

"We could take a break first," he said. "Go somewhere. Bali, maybe. Or the coast. Just the two of us."

She looked at him. He meant it kindly. She could see that he meant it kindly.

"Okay," she said.

He nodded, relieved, and came to sit beside her on the bed. He didn't touch her but he was there, close, and for a moment the room felt almost like it used to. She thought about saying something true. About the distance between *a meaningful change in variables* and what she was actually feeling, which had no variables and admitted no changes and sat in her chest like something calcified.

She didn't say it. She looked at the window instead.

After a while he went back downstairs, and she heard the laptop open again.

---

She found it that afternoon.

She had been avoiding the sketchbook since the night she pushed it to the end of the table, leaving it untouched while she worked from her laptop instead. But a client had requested hand-drawn concept sketches for a presentation and there was no avoiding it.

She brought it to the table and opened it.

The pages she had pushed past the cradles were still there, her client notes, the Collingwood floor plans, measurements in her own hand. She turned back toward the cradles slowly, the way you move toward something you already know is going to be worse than you left it.

They were worse.

Seven cradles, as before. But each one now held something. A shape beneath the blanket, the outline of something small and still, the suggestion of a form that had not been there when she last looked. She turned through them one by one. In the first the shape lay flat, barely visible. In the third it was more defined, the outline clearer, something that might have been a shoulder, the curve of a head. In the fifth it had shifted, turned slightly, as though in sleep.

In the seventh it was sitting up.

Facing the page. Facing whoever held the book.

Chel closed the sketchbook.

She sat with her hands flat on the cover for ten seconds, counting them, and then she opened it to the seventh page again and looked at it directly. The shape sat upright in the cradle with the patient stillness of something that had been waiting for her to look. The face was indistinct, smudged, not quite resolved. But the orientation was unmistakable. It was looking at her.

She tore the pages out. All seven, one after another, not carefully, the edges ragged where they came free. She carried them to the kitchen and dropped them in the bin under the sink, on top of the cloth she had used to wipe the footprint away, and pressed the lid down hard.

Then she stood at the kitchen counter and picked up her phone and called Mai.

---

Mai answered on the second ring.

"Ate? What's wrong?"

Chel had rehearsed something on the way to the phone, a measured version, something that would sound less like what it was. It didn't survive Mai's voice.

"Something is happening in the house," she said. "I don't know how to explain it. I need to tell someone."

She told her about the crying, how it had started the night of the IVF results, how it came from inside the walls, how it stopped the moment she sat up as though it knew she was listening. She told her about the footprint in the hallway, the smell of it, the way she had wiped it away without waking Jacob. She told her about the sketchbook, the cradles drawn in her hand that she had no memory of drawing, the shape that had appeared inside them, sitting up, looking at her.

Mai was quiet for a moment after she finished.

"How long has this been happening?"

"Since the results. Maybe before. I don't know."

"Have you told Jacob?"

"No."

Another silence. "Ate." Mai's voice was careful, feeling its way. "Do you think, I mean, is it possible this is the grief? That your mind is—"

"I touched the footprint," Chel said. "It was warm. It smelled like sour milk. I didn't imagine that."

Mai didn't push back. "Okay," she said quietly. "Okay."

They stayed on the line for a moment without speaking. Outside the kitchen window the garden was grey and still, the clean lines of it, the low-maintenance order she had designed into it. Nothing that needed too much tending.

"I've been thinking about Sunday," Chel said. She hadn't planned to say it. "Your choir. Mass."

Mai waited.

"I don't know if I—" Chel stopped. The sentence had several possible endings and none of them felt true enough to say aloud. *I don't know if I believe anything. I don't know if it would help. I don't know if I deserve to be there.*

"It's okay," Mai said softly. "Whenever you're ready. I'm not going anywhere."

Chel nodded, though Mai couldn't see it. "I should go," she said. "Jacob will be home soon."

"Call me," Mai said. "Any time. I mean it."

Chel set the phone down and stood in the quiet kitchen for a long moment. Through the ceiling she could hear nothing. The house was very still.

She went back to the bin and lifted the lid. The torn pages were there, ragged-edged, face down in the dark.

She closed the lid and went to make dinner.

---

That night she opened the sketchbook.

She had told herself she wouldn't. She had left it on the table when she went to bed and lain awake for an hour thinking about it before she got up, crossed the dark bedroom without turning on a light, and came downstairs.

The kitchen bin was closed. She didn't look in it.

She sat at the table and opened the sketchbook to the pages she had torn out.

They were there.

Restored exactly as they had been, the ragged edges gone, the pages whole and unbroken as though she had never touched them. Seven cradles. The shapes inside them. And the seventh, the figure sitting up, facing the page, was no longer sitting.

It was standing at the edge of the cradle. One foot on the floor, one still inside, caught in the act of climbing out. Its face still indistinct, still unresolved. But its body was turned outward now, away from the cradle, toward whatever lay beyond the page.

Toward her.

Chel closed the book. Turned off the kitchen light. Went back upstairs in the dark.

She lay down beside Jacob and looked at the ceiling and did not sleep and after a while she stopped trying. The house was quiet. Jacob's breathing was slow and even. Outside the street was empty, the tram lines dark, the city doing whatever the city did at three in the morning without her.

She found herself speaking. Not aloud, or barely aloud, her lips moving against the dark without sound. Not words exactly. More like the shape of words, the direction of them, aimed at something she couldn't name and couldn't locate and wasn't sure was there.

*I don't know who I'm talking to. I don't know if anyone is listening. But something is wrong and I don't know how to carry it anymore and I am asking. I don't know what I'm asking. I'm just asking.*

She stopped.

In the silence that followed, a word came up from somewhere without her meaning it. Not a name. Not a prayer exactly. Just the word her mother had always used for her, worn smooth with use, arriving now in the dark like something she hadn't known she was carrying.

*Anak.*

She didn't know who she was saying it to.

She lay with her eyes open and waited for something. She wasn't sure she expected anything.

She was looking at the ceiling when she saw it.

Not on the ceiling. In the corner of the room, low, where the wall met the floor. A shape. Crouched. The size of a small child, the limbs slightly wrong in the way she was beginning to recognize, the head bowed forward so the face was hidden.

Still. Completely still.

Chel did not move. Did not breathe. Her hands gripped the sheet on either side of her, knuckles tight, and she lay rigid and looked at the corner and the thing in the corner looked back.

Beside her Jacob slept on.

The figure did not move. It did not make a sound. It simply crouched in the corner of their bedroom with the patience of something that had nowhere else to be, in no hurry, content to wait for as long as waiting took.

Chel counted. She didn't know why. She counted to thirty and it was still there. She counted to sixty.

Then it was gone. Between one second and the next, the corner was empty and the room was just a room again, pale and dark and ordinary.

Chel lay still for a long time after that.

She did not wake Jacob.

She did not call Mai.

She lay in the dark with her hands still gripping the sheet and looked at the empty corner and understood, in the way you understand things at three in the morning that you will spend all day trying to unknow, that whatever was in the house with them was not confused about where it was.

It had come to the right place.

It had found exactly who it was looking for.
