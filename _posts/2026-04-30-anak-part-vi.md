## Part VI - The Return

> "Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me."
> *Psalm 23:4*

---

The heat hit them as they came through the arrivals gate, the particular humid weight of Philippine air that Chel had grown up in and never quite stopped missing, even when she told herself she hadn't missed it at all. The noise of it too, the voices, the horns outside, the density of everything, so different from Melbourne's ordered quiet that for a moment she just stood in the middle of it and let it press against her.

Jacob found her hand. She held on.

They took a bus north from the city, then a jeepney for the last hour into the province, the road narrowing and greening as they went, the mountains rising in the distance, the air changing as they climbed. Chel watched the landscape and felt something in her chest she couldn't name, some old recognition, the body remembering a place the mind had been trying to forget.

Her parents' house was at the end of a barangay road, a concrete house with a tiled roof and a garden her mother had been tending for thirty years. The smell of it, the sight of it, undid something in her before she had even opened the gate.

---

Isabel was at the door before they reached it.

She didn't say anything at first. She looked at Chel's face and opened her arms and Chel walked into them the way she had not allowed herself to walk into anything in years, without managing it, without holding herself carefully. She pressed her face against her mother's shoulder and her mother's hand came up to the back of her head, the gesture so old and so specific that something in Chel's chest cracked open along a line that had been forming for what felt like years.

Rodrigo stood in the doorway behind Isabel, upright as always, his face doing the thing it did when he was feeling something strongly and had decided not to make it about himself.

They went inside. The sala was neat and well-kept, framed family photographs along one wall, a corner altar with candles and a small statue of the Virgin, the kind of home that had always been maintained with quiet pride. Isabel made tea. Rodrigo sat across from them at the dining table where Chel had done her homework as a child, where the family had eaten together every night until the girls left for the city and then the world.

Chel told them.

She started with the pregnancy and didn't stop. The decision, the Philippines, the old woman, what they thought she was and what they understood now that she wasn't. The years of silence. The IVF. The haunting, the crying in the walls, the footprint, the sketchbook, the feeding, the date spoken in her own voice. She told it the way she had told Mai, haltingly and incompletely, circling back, finding the shape of it as she went.

Jacob sat beside her and did not speak and did not look away.

When she finished the room was very quiet. Outside the window the garden moved in a slight wind.

Isabel's eyes were wet. She had not looked away from Chel's face once.

"Mama," Chel said. Her voice broke on the word, the only word she had left.

Isabel reached across the table and took both her hands.

"Anak," she said.

Just that. The word that contained everything, the grief and the love and the long patience of a mother who had known something was wrong for years and had waited, and was still here, and would still be here. *Anak.* You are mine. You are still mine.

Chel broke.

Not quietly, not with the practiced steadiness she had been carrying since the clinic. She wept the way she had not wept in years, the sound of it coming up from somewhere below composure, below management, below everything she had been holding in place. Isabel pulled her in and held her, both arms around her, her mother's voice low and wordless against her hair. Rodrigo rose from his chair and stood behind them, his hand on Chel's shoulder, steady and present. Jacob sat beside her, not touching, not moving, just there, witnessing it the way he had learned to witness things on this journey, without trying to fix them.

She wept until there was nothing left to weep with. Then she was still, her face against her mother's shoulder, the room quiet around them.

Rodrigo made a sound. He pressed his hand flat on the table, eyes down, jaw working. He was not a man who cried easily. Tears were in his eyes now.

"We should have been there," he said. His voice was low. "You should have been able to come to us."

"I know," Chel said.

"You can always come to us," he said. "Whatever it is. Always."

They sat together in the grief of it for a long time, the four of them, not trying to fix it, not rushing toward resolution. Just present with what it was. Isabel's hands over Chel's. Rodrigo's palm flat on the table. Jacob beside her, his shoulder against hers.

Outside the garden moved in the wind.

---

Jacob's cousin Romy came for them at dusk.

He was a quiet man, a farmer, someone who had lived in this province his whole life and knew its contours the way Chel knew a floor plan. He had agreed to guide them without asking many questions, which suggested he already knew the answers. When they came out to meet him he looked at their faces and nodded once and got back in the truck without a word.

The road they took was unpaved after the first kilometer, threading into the foothills through cogon grass and secondary forest, the headlights cutting narrow tunnels in the dark. Romy drove with the particular careful attention of someone who knew this road and did not like it after dark.

"How long has she been here?" Jacob asked.

Romy was quiet for a moment. "*Matagal na,*" he said. *A long time.* "*Bago pa ang tatay ko. Bago pa ang lolo ko.*" *Before my father. Before his father.*

He said nothing else. The truck moved through the dark.

They smelled it before they saw it, something old and organic, sweet in a way that was wrong, the way sweetness is wrong when it comes from something decaying. Then the hut appeared in the headlights, low and dark at the edge of the tree line, its walls a patchwork of salvaged wood and rusted iron, firelight visible through the gaps in the boards.

Romy stopped the truck and kept the engine running. He did not turn it off.

"*Dito lang ako,*" he said. *I'll be right here.* He looked at Jacob. "*Mag-ingat kayo.*" *Be careful.*

Jacob nodded. He and Chel got out.

The night was very loud with insects. The hut sat at the edge of the headlights, the forest pressing close behind it. Chel felt the scapular against her skin and walked toward the door.

---

The woman inside was old.

Not frail, old in the way of things that have lasted a long time by being harder than what surrounds them. She sat on a low stool near an open cooking fire, her hands in her lap, and she looked at them when they entered with the expression of someone who had been expecting visitors without knowing precisely when they would arrive.

"Dumating kayo," she said. Her voice was flat, unhurried. *You came.*

The hut was small and close. Bundles of dried herbs and other things hung from the rafters, not all of them identifiable. Along one wall, a low shelf with objects Chel didn't look at directly, until her eyes found what sat at the end of it, small and pale, tiny bones, a skull the size of a clenched fist. She looked away. In the corner, marks on the wood that might have been a record of something, tallied in a hand she didn't recognize, row after row of them. The air was thick with the smell of the place, sweet and wrong, the smell that had reached them outside but concentrated now, intimate.

"Lift it," Jacob said. His voice was flat. "The kulam. *The curse*. Whatever you did to us. Lift it."

The old woman looked at him. Then she looked at Chel.

She took her time with it. Something moved in her eyes that was not quite recognition and not quite hunger but lived close to both.

Chel did not look away.

The old woman's expression did not change. When she spoke it was not to Jacob.

"*Sige nga,*" she said. *Go ahead then.*

She stood. She turned toward the far corner of the hut and she spoke, not Tagalog exactly, something older, something provincial and guttural that Chel had no register for, words that seemed to belong to a different kind of transaction entirely, spoken with the casual authority of someone who had said them many times before.

The temperature dropped.

The cooking fire guttered. The shadows thickened in the far corner and the thickening had a shape, and the shape resolved, and what stood in the corner was fully present, fully physical, filling the space with a weight and solidity the apparitions in Melbourne had never had.

It was beautiful.

That was the worst thing. For one suspended moment it stood in the firelight as a perfect child, a boy, handsome, a child that might have been theirs. It looked at Chel with eyes that were almost right and she felt her arms begin to move and then she felt something else.

The scapular against her skin. Warm. Present. The rough wool she had stopped noticing days ago, suddenly there, suddenly real, an anchor she hadn't known she needed until the moment she needed it.

She went still.

She looked at the beautiful thing in the firelight and she understood, with a clarity that had no drama in it, that her child was not here. Her child had never been here. This was something that had found the shape of her grief and worn it. Nothing more.

It was not her child.

The beautiful form fell away.

What replaced it was wrong in every particular. The head too large, swollen, the skull misshapen beneath a thin skin. The limbs too thin, the joints too prominent, bent at angles that suggested something assembled rather than born. Its feet were splayed wide, the toes spread like fingers, the nails long and dark and curved to points. Its hands were the same, thin, clawed, the fingers too many or too long, it was difficult to count them in the firelight. Its skin was the color of something kept from light. Its eyes were milky and wide and fixed on Chel with a focus that had no childhood in it.

It turned toward the old woman.

The old woman's composure broke for the first time. She said the words again, sharp and commanding, the summoning language that was supposed to direct it, to keep it in its lane, her voice rising with an urgency that cracked the performance of control she had maintained since they entered.

The tiyanak looked at her.

It crossed the small space between them before she could finish the sentence. One hand closed on her throat and the other found her shoulder and it pulled and what happened to her throat it did with its teeth, and the sound was wet and final and the old woman made one sound that was not a word and then no sound at all. She went down. The tiyanak stood over her with its mouth dark and running, the blood black in the firelight, and it looked at Chel with the milky eyes and the patience of something that has simply moved on to what it came for.

It charged.

Jacob stepped forward.

He met it in the middle of the hut with his whole body, the collision audible, and for a moment he held it, his arms locked around its thin frame, his teeth bared, his feet planted. He was bigger than it. Much bigger. And for a moment that seemed to matter. He felt its resistance and he felt his own strength against it and he thought he had a chance, that he could hold it, that this was something a man could do with his body if he committed to it entirely.

Then it clawed him across the shoulder.

The sound he made drove into Chel's chest like a blade. Four lines opened in his shirt and the flesh beneath it and the blood came immediately, dark and fast. He didn't let go. He held on, bleeding, feet dragging backward across the dirt floor, and he looked at Chel over its shoulder with the clear eyes he had in the car when he said *we need to go back.*

The tiyanak released him and flung him. He crossed the width of the hut and hit the far wall with a sound that was all wrong and slid down it and did not get up immediately.

The tiyanak turned to Chel.

It moved toward her and she stood her ground and when it reached her and its clawed hands found her arms she felt a child's strength. That was all. The grip of something small, something that could be held, the thin fingers pressing into her skin with a force she recognized as manageable. She felt the scapular warm against her chest and she understood. *Grace*. It was already there. Waiting for her.

She wrapped her arms around it.

It writhed against her, the claws finding purchase on her arms, and she turned and drove it into the cooking fire.

The sound it made was not a child's sound and she would not think about it afterward if she could help it. The fire caught immediately, the creature and then the dry bundles on the rafters above, the flame traveling faster than seemed possible, and the heat was sudden and total.

"Chel—"

Jacob. On his feet, shoulder dark with blood, already moving toward her. She turned from the fire and a beam came down from the rafters and caught her hard across the back and drove her to her knees, the heat pressing on all sides, the air thinning.

His hand found her arm. He pulled.

They went through the door and into the night, stumbling, Jacob half-carrying her, and the hut behind them was fully alight, the fire reaching into the dark sky above the tree line, orange and enormous.

They made it to the grass and stopped. Chel's back burned where the beam had caught her. Jacob was breathing in ragged pulls, the claw marks across his shoulder dark in the firelight, his arm hanging at a wrong angle.

Romy was already out of the truck, running toward them. He got an arm under Jacob, looked at Chel, looked at the fire. He stood for a moment with the burning hut behind him and he looked down the dark road they had come from, toward the province, toward the other roads that threaded into other foothills, other trees. He thought of the women who had come before Chel. Of the ones who had not come back. Of the other huts in other provinces, the ones still standing.

He turned back to them.

"*Halika na,*" he said. *Let's go.*

He helped Jacob to the truck. Chel walked behind them, her back aching, the scapular warm against her skin, the fire loud behind her.

She did not look back.
