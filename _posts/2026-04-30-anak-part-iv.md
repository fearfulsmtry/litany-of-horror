**Part IV - The Feast**

*"He gives the barren woman a home, making her the joyous mother of children."*
*Psalm 113:9*

---

Tonio's backyard was loud before they even opened the gate.

Children's voices, high and formless, the kind of noise that spills over fences and doesn't apologize for itself. The smell of charcoal and pork fat already in the air, something caramelizing somewhere, the sharp sweetness of banana ketchup on the grill. Jacob held the gate open and Chel stepped through and the party received them the way Filipino parties always did, immediately and completely, as though they had been expected and their absence until this moment had been a minor administrative error now corrected.

"Jakey! Finally!" Tonio at the grill, tongs raised in greeting, apron already dark with grease. Beside him Eduardo held a beer, the other hand resting on the shoulder of a small boy who looked exactly like Tonio had looked twenty years ago. Three other children orbited the adults at various speeds, one of them trailing a plastic dinosaur by its tail, another already angling toward the dessert table with the focused intensity of someone who had done this before.

The table was already overwhelming. Trays of pancit tangled with shrimp and pork, the noodles glossy and fragrant. A whole lechon in the center, skin blistered amber, the smell of it enough to make you forget everything. Kare-kare in a clay pot, thick and golden, the oxtail soft from hours of cooking. Lumpia stacked in rows, their edges crisp and dark. Rice cookers humming at the end of the table, lids steaming. Halo-halo sweating in tall glasses, the shaved ice already beginning to soften in the afternoon heat. Plates of ensaladang talong, the eggplant charred and silky, dressed with vinegar and onion. And more coming, the titas moving between kitchen and table with the efficient choreography of women who had fed large groups their entire lives, arriving with platters and departing with empty ones, pausing only to pinch a cheek or redirect a child.

Cecilia appeared from the kitchen doorway with a bowl of dinuguan, pressing it into Chel's hands before she had fully said hello. "Anak, you're thin. Eat. Both of you, eat first, talk after."

She was swept into it then. That was the thing about these parties, you didn't attend them so much as get absorbed into them. Someone put a plate in her hands. Someone else pulled her toward the table. A tita she hadn't seen in two years gripped both her cheeks and said something about how beautiful she was and why weren't there babies yet, and before Chel could answer the tita had been redirected by a child demanding attention and the moment dissolved.

She found a seat at the long table and sat with her plate and watched.

The yard was full of children. That was what she kept coming back to. Not abstractly full, not a few children playing quietly in a corner, but genuinely, abundantly full. A toddler in a white dress was being passed between lolas like a very small celebrity, each one pressing their lips to her forehead in turn. Two boys of about seven were conducting a negotiation over a bag of chips that would clearly end in tears. A baby in a bouncer near the kitchen doorway was receiving a steady stream of visitors, each one crouching down to make faces and receive a wet smile in return.

Chel ate without tasting.

Beside her one of the titas was telling a story about a neighbor's renovation. At the other end of the table Eduardo was talking to Tonio about something that made them both laugh, the sound large and easy. Jacob stood with the men near the grill, stubby in hand, nodding at the right moments. He had the slightly delayed quality of someone performing a role they knew well but had stopped fully inhabiting, his laugh a half-beat behind everyone else's.

She watched him and thought: he is doing exactly what I am doing.

A small hand landed on her knee. She looked down. A girl of about three, round-faced, regarding her with the frank assessment of someone who had not yet learned to pretend. She was holding a piece of lechon skin in one fist.

"Hi," the girl said.

"Hi," Chel said.

The girl considered her for another moment, then moved on, apparently satisfied. Chel watched her go and felt something tighten in her chest that she did not examine.

---

She didn't see it happen. She only saw Jacob afterward, standing slightly apart from the men near the fence, a fresh stubby untouched in his hand, staring at nothing in particular with the expression of someone who had just been handed something heavy and wasn't sure where to put it.

Cecilia was already back at the table, refilling a child's plate, unremarkable. But she had a way of moving through a party that covered a great deal of ground.

---

The afternoon shifted toward evening. The grill coals glowed lower. Someone strung lights above the table. Children who had been running began to droop, claimed by lolas and deposited in laps. The conversation quieted into something more comfortable, the loud sociability of midafternoon softening into the easier warmth of people who had eaten well and were in no hurry to leave.

Lola Coring had been sitting at the corner of the table for most of the evening, watching rather than talking, the way very old people sometimes do at parties, taking everything in with the unhurried attention of someone who knows their time at such gatherings is not unlimited. She was Eduardo's mother, small and birdlike, with the kind of face that had probably always looked old and wise, a face that had been waiting since youth to grow into itself.

At some point she began to speak, and the children who were still awake and nearby drifted toward her the way children do toward the old ones who tell stories, drawn by something older than reason.

"In the province," she said, her Tagalog soft and unhurried, "we did not call certain things by their names after dark." She paused to let a child settle at her feet. "There were things that could hear you. Things that were waiting to be called."

One of the titas made a sound of affectionate protest. "Lola, the children—"

"The children should know." Lola Coring's eyes did not move from the glowing coals. "Better to know than to be surprised."

She told them about the aswang first, the shapeshifters, the ones who flew at night with the sound of a beating heart. She told them about the duwende, the small ones who lived under old trees and did not forgive carelessness. The children leaned closer. One of the boys had stopped eating entirely.

"But the one to fear most," Lola Coring said, her voice dropping, "is the one that wears the face of what you love."

The yard was very quiet. Even the adults had stopped talking.

"The tiyanak," she said. "They say it is the spirit of a child taken before its time. Before baptism. Before a name." She paused. "But that is not quite right. What it is, is an old hunger wearing a young face. It finds the ones who are already broken open. The ones with an empty place inside them. And it fills that place."

A child whimpered softly. A tita reached for her.

"How do you stop it?" the boy asked.

Lola Coring looked at him for a long moment. "You name it for what it is," she said. "And you close the door it came through."

She said nothing more. Someone changed the subject, the conversation restarting with the determined cheerfulness of people who had heard enough. The children were redistributed. Jacob caught Chel's eye from across the table and she looked away.

---

She found herself alone in the kitchen twenty minutes later, standing at the sink, running water she wasn't using over her hands.

The kitchen was warm and smelled of everything that had been cooked in it, layers of it, garlic and pork fat and something sweet. Through the window she could see the yard, the lights strung above the table, the shapes of people she had known most of her life moving through the dark.

She heard the small sound of a cane on tile and turned.

Lola Coring stood in the kitchen doorway, one hand on the frame, regarding her with the same unhurried attention she had given the party all evening.

"You weren't listening to a story," the old woman said. It was not a question.

Chel turned the tap off. "No."

Lola Coring moved into the kitchen slowly, the cane finding the floor with each step, and stopped beside her at the counter. She didn't look at Chel. She looked at the window, at the yard, at the people moving through the dark outside.

"Something follows you," she said quietly.

Chel's hands were still on the edge of the sink. She didn't answer.

The old woman reached into the neck of her blouse and drew out a cord. On the cord hung two small pieces of dark brown wool, rough-edged, stitched together, the size of a large postage stamp. She lifted it over her head and held it out.

Chel looked at it.

"Brown scapular," Lola Coring said. "I have worn it since I was a girl. Our Lady's promise." She pressed it into Chel's hand and closed her fingers around it, her grip surprisingly firm. "Keep this, iha."

Chel felt the weight of it in her palm. Rough wool, warm from the old woman's skin.

"Lola—"

"You don't have to believe in it yet," the old woman said. "It will wait."

She patted Chel's closed fist once, then turned and made her way back toward the doorway, the cane finding the tile. At the threshold she paused but did not look back.

"Close the door, iha," she said. "Whatever it came through."

Then she was gone, back into the noise and warmth of the party.

Chel stood in the kitchen with the scapular in her closed fist and the sound of the party around her and said nothing to no one.

After a while she put it in her pocket and went back outside.

---

They drove home in silence.

Not the cold silence of the months before, the silence that had learned to take up space between them. This was different, quieter, a silence that had something in it neither of them had named yet. The city moved past the windows. Chel watched it and turned the scapular over in her pocket, the rough wool between her fingers.

Halfway home she asked: "What did mum say to you?"

Jacob kept his eyes on the road. "She said she could see I was carrying something." A pause. "She said the things we carry alone get heavier. Not lighter."

Chel looked at his profile in the dark.

"She said God doesn't ask us to be strong," he said. "He asks us to be honest."

The city moved past the windows. Neither of them spoke again.

The townhouse was dark when they arrived. Jacob turned the lights on while Chel hung her jacket and felt the weight of the evening settling into her, the food and the noise and the children and Lola's hand closing around hers. She was tired in a way that went below the body.

"I'm going to shower," Jacob said.

"Okay."

She went upstairs. Changed into her nightdress. Lay down in the dark without turning on the lamp, the scapular on the bedside table beside her phone, and closed her eyes.

---

Jacob stood at the bathroom mirror with the tap running, not using it.

The party had left something in him he couldn't name. Lola Coring's words threading through him without permission, the children in the yard, the look on Chel's face when the small girl put her hand on her knee. Cecilia's words in the dark by the fence. *The things we carry alone get heavier. Not lighter.*

He was getting ready for bed, toothbrush in hand, when he stopped and looked at his own reflection. The familiar geography of his own face looking back at him. He had been standing here longer than he realized.

He became aware that he was not alone in the mirror.

The reflection was wrong. His own face, his own bathroom, his own hands. But in the space behind him, in the dark beyond his shoulder, a shape. Small. The size of a child, the head slightly too large, the face pale and wide-eyed. Looking at him with an expression he could not name and did not want to.

He did not move.

The shape's mouth opened.

When it spoke the voice was not his. Close to his, almost his, the way a recording sounds like you and doesn't. But the words were his exactly, the cadence of them his, the exact register he used in the careful arguments he made for things he had already decided.

*We're building the future. There will be time. It's the right call.*

His own words in a voice he almost recognized. The thing that had lived in his chest the morning he made the decision and that he had carried ever since without examining.

The shape smiled. The teeth were sharp and small. A thin line of blood appeared at the corner of its mouth and ran down slowly, darkly, in the silence.

He could not look away. He could not move. The blood reached the shape's chin and hung there and the smile did not waver and his own face looked back at him from the mirror and he stood between them, unable to move, unable to—

The scream came from the bedroom.

---

*Earlier.*

She had been almost asleep when the warmth woke her.

She surfaced slowly, the way you do from the edge of sleep, the body registering something before the mind catches up. A warmth at her breast, pressing, rhythmic. Familiar in the way that the most wrong things can be familiar, because the body knows the shape of what it was made for even when what is pressing is not that thing.

She lay still for a moment, not yet fully awake.

Then she was awake.

She lay rigid, eyes open in the dark, the warmth still there, the pressure still rhythmic. She could not see anything. The room was dark, the lamp off, the streetlight thin at the edges of the curtains.

She reached for the lamp.

The light came on.

She looked down.

Her nightdress was dark at the breast, the fabric clinging damp and warm against her skin. She pulled it aside with shaking hands.

The marks were small. Two crescents pressed into the skin above her breast, the shape of a bite, deep enough that the blood had soaked into the cotton and was still spreading, still warm, the heat of it against her fingers the most terrible thing, that it was still warm, that whatever had made these marks had been here moments ago and she had not known, she had been sleeping and it had been—

She looked up.

At the foot of the bed, in the shadows where the lamplight didn't reach, a shape. Small. Still. Standing in the way it stood, the proportions almost right, the face still in shadow. Its mouth moved.

When it spoke, the voice was hers. Her own voice, her own pitch, the exact way she sounded to herself in recordings, the voice she had never quite been able to recognize as her own.

"December twenty-eighth."

She screamed.

---

He hit the doorframe going through, shoulder hard against the wood, barely felt it. The lamp was on. Chel was upright in the bed, nightdress pulled aside, her hand pressed over her breast, her face stripped of everything that was not terror.

He crossed the room and reached for her and this time when his hands found her she did not step away. She gripped him. Her fingers closed on his t-shirt and she held on and he held her and she shook and he shook and outside the window the street was empty and silent and the city went on without them, indifferent and ordinary, as it always did.

He did not look at the foot of the bed.

He did not look at the mirror in the hall as they sat through the long hours of what remained of the night.

He held his wife in the ruined dark and for the first time in longer than he could account for, he had no protocol for what came next.
