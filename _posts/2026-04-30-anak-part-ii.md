**Part II - Cradles**

*"I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint; my heart is like wax, it is melted within my breast."*
*Psalm 22:14*

---

Mai's apartment was in Brunswick, a fifteen-minute tram ride from the townhouse that felt like crossing into a different kind of life.

The door opened before Chel could knock. Mai had been watching for her from the window, the way she always did, and there she was, her younger sister, bright-faced, already reaching for her, arms tight around her shoulders, holding on a beat longer than a greeting required. She smelled of the same shampoo she'd used since university. Chel pressed her face briefly into her sister's shoulder and let herself be held.

"Come in, come in. I made arroz caldo. Sit down."

The apartment was small and warm and full of the particular disorder of a life actively lived in. Nursing textbooks stacked beside a half-finished jigsaw on the coffee table. A string of fairy lights above the kitchen window. Photographs on the refrigerator held up with mismatched magnets, cousins and friends and a shot of the two of them from a family Christmas three years ago, Chel laughing at something off-camera, her face open in a way she couldn't quite remember being. A small wooden cross above the door, the kind their mother had put in every room of the family home in the province.

Mai moved around the kitchen with the comfortable efficiency of someone used to feeding people on short notice, ladling soup into bowls, slicing ginger, talking as she went.

"How's Jacob?"

"Working."

Mai set a bowl in front of her and sat across the table, tucking her feet beneath her. She had been watching Chel's face since the door. "The results came back?"

Chel looked at the soup. The steam rose thin and straight in the still air of the apartment.

"The IVF failed," she said. "Total fertilization failure. No embryos."

Mai went very still. Then she reached across the table and covered Chel's hand with both of hers.

She didn't fill the space immediately, the way others might, rushing to cover the silence before it could settle. She just sat with it for a moment, her hands warm over Chel's, letting the news be what it was.

Then, quietly: "I'm so sorry, Ate."

Chel nodded. She had been holding herself carefully since the clinic, the way you carry something full without spilling it. Something in Mai's kitchen was making that harder.

Mai's eyes had gone bright. She blinked it back quickly, the way she did when she didn't want to make something about herself. "I really wanted to be a tita," she said, almost to herself. Then, softer: "I mean. I still will be. Someday."

It was said with such uncomplicated faith that Chel didn't know whether to laugh or cry.

"The doctor said we could try again. Jacob wants to try again."

"What do you want?"

The question arrived simply, without agenda. Chel looked at her sister.

"I don't know," she said. "I think I'm tired."

Mai nodded, not rushing to fill the silence. Outside a tram bell rang, faint and distant. The fairy lights above the window cast a small warm glow across the bench.

They ate the arroz caldo. Mai talked about small things between the silences, a difficult patient, Jed coming for dinner on Saturday, a friend from youth ministry getting engaged. Not to distract, but to let Chel surface and breathe when she needed to. It was a skill Chel had always admired in her sister without being able to name until now: the ability to be present without demanding anything from the presence.

At some point Mai said, as naturally as she might mention the weather: "I've been praying for you both. For a while now."

Chel looked up.

"Every morning," Mai said simply. "I just wanted you to know."

Chel felt something move in her chest, not quite emotion, not quite relief. Something smaller and less categorizable than either. She thought about her mother's hand reaching toward the camera. She thought about the dark ceiling of the bedroom and the attention she had aimed at something she couldn't name.

"Thank you," she said. It came out more quietly than she intended.

Mai refilled her bowl and was quiet for a moment. Then she looked at Chel with that particular expression, the one that meant she was about to say something their mother would say. She put on the voice, soft and deliberate: *"Anak."* Then, dropping it: "You know she'd say that right now. You know she'd be on the next flight."

Chel almost smiled. "She'd bring lumpia."

"She'd bring enough lumpia for the whole street."

Then, carefully, without pressure: "I know it's been a while. But our group leads choir every Sunday afternoon at Mass. You could come with me if you wanted. Visit once even. No pressure."

Chel looked at her hands around the bowl.

The offer sat in the air between them, open and unhurried. She felt the pull of it, faint but real, the way you feel the warmth of a room before you've decided to enter it. Mai wasn't asking her to believe anything or fix anything. Just to be somewhere, in a space that had been built for exactly this kind of weight.

"I'll think about it," Chel said.

It wasn't yes. But it wasn't the no she had expected to give.

Mai smiled and asked nothing further.

---

It was dark by the time Chel got home.

The townhouse received her with its usual silence. Jacob's keys on the counter, the faint sound of movement upstairs, his voice low on a call. She stood in the entrance hall for a moment, the warmth of Mai's apartment still faintly on her skin, and felt the contrast like a change in temperature.

She made tea she didn't drink. Stood at the kitchen window looking at the narrow strip of garden below, the one she had landscaped herself three years ago. Clean lines. Low maintenance. Nothing that needed too much tending.

After a while she took her sketchbook to the table.

She had a client brief to work through, a young couple in Collingwood wanting an open-plan renovation. She opened the book to the last page she remembered working on and found her notes from the site visit, measurements, a rough floor plan, annotations in her own hand.

She turned the page.

A cradle. The same one she had found the night before, or one like it, the same proportions, the same careful canopy. She turned again. Another. And another after that, each one drawn in her hand, her style, filling pages she had left blank for client work. Some were near identical. Others had small variations, a different curve to the hood, the bars spaced slightly wider, as though whoever had drawn them had been working through iterations, refining something toward a final form.

She counted seven before she stopped counting.

Chel sat very still with the book open in her hands. The drawings were unhesitating, each one made with the same quiet confidence as the first. Not the work of someone distressed or dreaming. The work of someone who knew exactly what they were making and had made it many times before.

She closed the book.

She sat at the table with her hands flat on the cover for a long moment. Then she pushed it to the other end of the table, as far from her as the surface allowed, and sat looking at it from a distance as though that might help.

It didn't.

She left it there and went upstairs to bed.

---

The crying woke her just after two.

Clearer than before. Not from the street, not from the neighboring terrace. From inside, and closer this time, as though whatever made the sound had moved further into the house since the last time she had heard it.

Jacob slept on, one arm flung across the pillow above his head, breathing slow and even.

Chel lay still for a moment, waiting to see if it would stop the way it had before. It didn't. It continued, thin and wavering, a sound at the edge of what a newborn's cry should be, close enough to recognize and wrong enough to make the recognition terrible.

She got up.

The hallway was dark, the bathroom door ajar, the pale glow of the streetlight coming through the window at the end of the corridor. She stood and listened. The crying had stopped when she left the bedroom, the same way it had stopped when she sat up the first time. She was beginning to understand that this was part of it.

She stood in the hallway for a long moment, heart loud in the dark.

Then she looked down.

Just outside the bathroom door, on the pale timber floorboards she had chosen and laid herself, a small print. The size of a toddler's foot, perhaps smaller. The toes were too widely spaced, the shape slightly wrong at the heel, as though whatever had made it was only approximately the right shape.

Chel crouched down slowly.

She touched the edge of it with one finger. It was damp. She brought her finger to her nose before she could think to stop herself.

Sour. Faintly warm. The smell of milk gone wrong.

She stood up. Went to the kitchen, took a cloth from beneath the sink, came back and wiped the print away with careful, deliberate strokes, corner to corner, until the floorboard was clean and dry and there was no evidence of anything. Then she dropped the cloth in the bin under the sink, not the laundry, and stood with her hands braced on the counter for a moment.

She didn't wake Jacob.

She didn't call Mai.

She went back to bed and lay in the dark and did not sleep and did not speak and after a while the grey of early morning came to the curtains the same as it had before, patient and indifferent, as though nothing in the night had been worth noting.

On the dining table below, the sketchbook sat at the far end of the table where she had pushed it.

Lying in the dark, she found herself wondering how many pages were filled while she slept.
