**Epilogue - Anak**

*"He heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds."*
*Psalm 147:3*

---

The church was in Carlton, a ten-minute walk from the townhouse, the kind of parish that had been there longer than the suburb around it and wore that fact quietly. They went on a Saturday morning, together, and sat in the same pew before taking their turns.

She sat in the dim of the confessional and said what she had come to say. The priest on the other side of the grille was old, his breathing audible in the silence, and he listened without interrupting. The wood smelled of decades of the same thing being said and received and forgiven. When she finished he was quiet for a moment.

Then he spoke. His voice was unhurried, the voice of someone who had heard the worst things people carry and had not yet been surprised into losing his composure. He gave her her penance. She said her act of contrition with her hands pressed together and her eyes closed and meant every word of it, which she had not been sure she would.

She stepped out of the booth into the body of the church.

Jacob was already there, on his knees in a pew near the front, head bowed, lips moving slightly. She walked toward him and she did not announce herself, just knelt beside him in the same pew, close enough that their shoulders touched, and bowed her own head.

They stayed like that for a long time.

Afterward they lit a candle together at the side altar, one of the small ones in the red glass holders, the flame catching immediately and steadying. They stood in front of it for a moment, side by side, Jacob's hand finding hers. Neither of them said anything. There was nothing that needed to be said. The candle was enough, a small warm light for the child that had no name and no grave, the child that was with God and always had been, the child they had spent years not being able to look at directly and could look at now, together, in the light.

They walked home through Carlton with the morning around them, the trams and the coffee shops and the ordinary Saturday world, and Chel felt the particular quality of the air on her skin and thought: *this is what it feels like when the house has been cleaned.*

---

Three weeks later she was back at work.

A young couple in Fitzroy, their first home together, wanting something that felt like themselves rather than a catalogue. Chel had taken the brief and felt, for the first time in longer than she could account for, the particular pleasure of a space that wanted to become something. She visited the site on a Tuesday morning, made her notes, measured the light.

That afternoon she sat at the dining table with her sketchbook open and drew.

Not the Fitzroy apartment. Just something for herself, the way she used to draw before drawing became tangled up with everything else. A facade she liked the proportions of. A window detail she had been thinking about. Her pencil moved the way it used to move, unhurried and sure, the line going where she intended it to go.

She filled three pages before she noticed the time.

She closed the sketchbook and left it on the table and went to make dinner. Jacob came home at seven, earlier than usual, and she heard the key in the lock and felt the particular warmth of a house that had someone coming into it.

---

She sat with the knowledge for a day without saying anything, the way you hold something fragile, turning it carefully, making sure of it before you let it be real. Then she went to the pharmacy on Brunswick Street and walked home with the small box in her bag and went upstairs while Jacob was in his office on a call.

The bathroom was quiet. The tiles cool underfoot. Morning light through the frosted window.

She sat on the edge of the bath and waited.

The test sat on the vanity in front of her. She looked at it the way she had looked at many things in this bathroom: the footprint she had wiped away in the dark, the mirror Jacob had not wanted to look at, the tiles she had chosen herself because she had thought once that a beautiful house was the same as a full one.

Two lines. Positive.

She read the word and read it again, as though it might change between one reading and the next.

She sat very still for a long time. Her hands were in her lap. The morning light moved slightly as a cloud passed outside and the bathroom went briefly dim and came back again. Somewhere below, Jacob's call had ended, and she could hear him moving through the house, the familiar sound of him, the particular weight of his footsteps.

She was not crying. She was something beyond crying, something that had no name yet, something that would take time to become itself.

She looked at the two lines and she felt the scapular against her skin, still there, still warm, and she thought of Lola Coring's hand closing around hers in a kitchen that smelled of the party, *you don't have to believe in it yet, it will wait,* and of her mother's hand reaching toward a screen twelve thousand kilometers away, and of Mai's hands covering hers across a small table, and of Jacob's hands laced through hers in the warm Philippine dark while the fire did its work behind them.

All those hands.

She looked at the test and she placed her own hand flat against her stomach, the gesture so old in her by now it arrived without thought, and she said the word quietly, into the still air of the bathroom, to herself and to the life beginning inside her and to whatever was listening.

*Anak.*
